# Before You Collect Opening-Protection Quotes Under My Safe Florida Home

Use this brief if your report already points toward opening protection and you want to avoid turning one recommendation into a much bigger project too early.

This brief is not the program, does not approve grants, and does not confirm reimbursement. It is a narrow pre-quote guide based on the report language and current program-facing rules.

## What this quote step is for
At this step, the goal is not to price every possible upgrade.

The goal is to:

- confirm whether your report is really pointing to opening protection
- identify what kind of contractor type fits first
- gather the few items needed for a disciplined first quote

## Check these parts of the report first
Before you ask for a quote, look at:

1. the exact recommended-improvements wording
2. any 1802 summary or wind-mitigation language that explains why the recommendation exists
3. any note that narrows scope to certain openings instead of the whole house

If the wording is still unclear, do not let the first salesperson decide the scope for you.

## What to send before you ask for a quote
Send only the items that help the contractor stay inside the recommendation:

1. the report page that shows the recommendation
2. your home type:
   - detached
   - attached or townhouse-like
3. your county or ZIP code
4. photos of the openings you think are involved
5. any note that suggests some openings are already compliant or excluded

## What opening-protection scope usually means at this stage
At this stage, a disciplined quote should stay focused on:

- which openings are actually in scope
- whether the first path is shutters, windows, doors, garage-door protection, or a narrower mix
- which documents, permits, and proof items may matter later

It should not automatically widen into unrelated roofing work or a whole-house replacement story.

## Attached-home caution
If your home is attached or townhouse-like, verify scope early.

Under current program-facing materials, attached-home cases may narrow more sharply than detached-home cases. Do not assume detached-home advice applies without checking.

## What not to assume
- do not assume every opening needs the same product
- do not assume the most expensive quote is the safest quote
- do not assume a contractor mentioning the program means the scope is correct
- do not assume a broad whole-house package is the same thing as the recommendation in your report

## Questions to carry into the first quote
Ask these before you compare price:

1. Which exact recommendation from my report is this quote solving?
2. Which openings are inside this quote, and which are outside it?
3. What part of this quote is required for the mitigation path, and what part is optional?
4. Who handles permits, inspections, and closeout documents?
5. If my home is attached, what keeps this quote inside the narrower scope?

## Best next step
Use this brief to narrow the first quote request.

If the first quote still feels broad, ask for the scope to be restated opening by opening before you compare price.
